\begin{abstract}
Medical image classification demands not only high accuracy but also reliable uncertainty estimation to identify out-of-distribution (OOD) samples that may lead to dangerous misdiagnoses. We present MedGuard, a hybrid framework that combines deep learning feature extraction with Gaussian Mixture Models for uncertainty-aware classification. Our approach fine-tunes a ResNet18 backbone on PathMNIST (9-class colon pathology), extracts 512-dimensional embeddings, and fits a GMM to model the training distribution. At inference time, samples with log-likelihood below the 5th percentile threshold are flagged as OOD. We compare three approaches---HOG+SVM baseline, fine-tuned ResNet18, and our hybrid GMM---demonstrating that the hybrid approach maintains competitive classification accuracy while providing principled uncertainty estimates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Automated pathology image classification has the potential to reduce diagnostic workload and improve consistency. However, clinical deployment requires models that know when they don't know---i.e., models that can detect inputs outside their training distribution and abstain from making potentially harmful predictions.

We address this through a three-pronged approach:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{baseline ML} model using handcrafted features (HOG) with an SVM classifier, establishing a non-deep-learning reference point.
    \item A \textbf{deep learning} model (fine-tuned ResNet18) optimized with class-weighted loss and early stopping.
    \item A \textbf{hybrid GMM} model that fits a Gaussian Mixture Model on the deep learning embeddings, enabling principled out-of-distribution detection.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
Out-of-distribution detection in medical imaging has been explored through Monte Carlo Dropout \cite{gal2016dropout}, deep ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple}, and density-based methods. Our GMM-based approach follows the density estimation paradigm, similar to Mahalanobis distance methods \cite{lee2018simple}, but uses a full mixture model to capture multi-modal embedding distributions.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset}
PathMNIST from the MedMNIST benchmark contains 107,180 colon pathology images at $28 \times 28$ resolution across 9 tissue types.

\subsection{Baseline: HOG + SVM}
We extract Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) features from images resized to $64 \times 64$. The SVM with RBF kernel computes the decision function:
\begin{equation}
    f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_s} \alpha_i y_i K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}) + b
\end{equation}
where the RBF kernel is:
\begin{equation}
    K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \exp\left(-\gamma \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'\|^2\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deep Learning: ResNet18}
We fine-tune a pretrained ResNet18 with a modified classifier head:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{W} \cdot \text{Dropout}(\mathbf{z}) + \mathbf{b})
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{512}$ is the penultimate layer output. Training uses class-weighted cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = -\sum_{c=1}^{C} w_c \cdot y_c \log(\hat{y}_c), \quad w_c = \frac{N}{C \cdot N_c}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hybrid GMM for OOD Detection}
We model the embedding distribution using a Gaussian Mixture Model:
\begin{equation}
    p(\mathbf{z}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \cdot \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)
\end{equation}
The OOD score for a test sample is its log-likelihood under the GMM:
\begin{equation}
    s(\mathbf{z}) = \log p(\mathbf{z})
\end{equation}
The OOD threshold $\tau$ is set to the 5th percentile of training scores:
\begin{equation}
    \tau = Q_{0.05}\left(\{s(\mathbf{z}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N_{\text{train}}}\right)
\end{equation}
A sample is flagged as OOD if $s(\mathbf{z}) < \tau$.

\section{Conclusion}
MedGuard demonstrates that combining deep learning embeddings with Gaussian Mixture Models provides a practical framework for uncertainty-aware medical image classification. The hybrid approach maintains competitive accuracy while enabling principled out-of-distribution detection---a critical capability for clinical deployment.
